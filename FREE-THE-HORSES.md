# Free the Horses — Campaign Plan

The World Horse Organization's first real-world initiative: end horse-drawn carriages in New York City's Central Park and transition the industry to electric alternatives.

---

## Why this matters

Horses are prey animals forced to work in one of the most congested cities on earth. They haul heavy carriages through Midtown traffic, breathe car exhaust, pound pavement for hours, and have no access to pasture or herd life. When they spook — and they do, regularly — people get hurt.

This isn't a fringe position. A 2022 poll found **71% of NYC voters oppose horse-drawn carriages**. Major cities worldwide — Beijing, New Delhi, Paris, London, Palm Beach, Biloxi — have already banned them. NYC is behind.

---

## The case

### Animal welfare

- Horses work up to 9-hour days in extreme heat and cold.
- They're deprived of pasture, herd interaction, and natural movement.
- The commute from Hell's Kitchen stables to Central Park runs through some of the most dangerous streets in the city.
- Named horses who have died or been injured in recent years: Ryder, Shadow, Aysha, Lily Rose, Spotty, Smoothie, Juliet, Jackie, Lucky, Monty, Bobby, Billy, Freddy, and Luciana.

### Public safety

- In May 2025 alone, three horses spooked and bolted in Central Park, injuring at least four people.
- Horses are prey animals. Blinders help but cannot eliminate the startle response. No regulation can prevent a spook.
- E-bikes, scooters, and increasing pedestrian density make the environment more dangerous every year.

### It's archaic

- The carriage concession made sense when Central Park was designed in the 1850s. The city is a different place now.
- Every argument for keeping them is rooted in nostalgia, not in the welfare of the animals.

## Opposition arguments and responses

These come from the Reddit thread, industry supporters, and TWU messaging. We need to answer every one of them clearly.

| Opposition argument | Response |
|---|---|
| "The horses are well cared for — they get vet checks, mandatory rest, and 5 weeks of vacation." | Regulated cruelty is still cruelty. You can't regulate away the fundamental problem: a prey animal hauling weight through Midtown traffic. The "vacation" is a marketing term for mandatory downtime at upstate farms — often the same farms where horses are rotated in and out of service. |
| "These horses would be slaughtered if not for this industry. Many are rescues from racing or Amish country." | If the industry's defense is "we're better than the slaughterhouse," the bar is underground. Ryder's Law includes provisions requiring horses be retired to sanctuaries, not auctioned. Multiple rescue organizations have pledged capacity. |
| "It's a living, breathing piece of NYC history. Olmsted designed the park for carriages." | Olmsted designed the park in 1858 when horses were the primary mode of transportation. He didn't design it for tourist concessions alongside 21st-century traffic. History evolves. |
| "What about the 300 jobs?" | The plan explicitly includes job transition. Drivers would operate electric carriages — same routes, same tips, same tourist experience, without the animal. This is about upgrading the job, not eliminating it. |
| "This is a land grab by real estate developers who want the stable property." | The WHO campaign is not affiliated with any real estate interest. We want the horses freed, not the stables sold. If stables can be repurposed as community space or electric carriage depots, even better. |
| "The horses seem fine. They're calm and well-fed." | Learned helplessness looks like calm. These are herd animals denied everything natural to them — grazing, open movement, social bonding with other horses. "Not visibly suffering" is not the same as thriving. |
| "What happens to the horses if they ban it?" | Sanctuary placement. Multiple organizations have committed. Ryder's Law includes mandatory retirement provisions. The WHO will fundraise specifically for this — see our plan below. |
| "The union (TWU) will fight it." | TWU Local 100 claims to represent the drivers, but the drivers are independent contractors with no collective bargaining agreement or union benefits. The union's real concern appears to be political leverage, not worker welfare. We should be allies to the workers by offering them a better job (electric carriages), not enemies. |

## Plan for the horses

### Immediate (if ban passes)

1. **Mandatory sanctuary retirement.** No horse goes to auction. Ryder's Law already includes this provision — we support and help enforce it.
2. **WHO fundraising for placement costs.** Sanctuary intake isn't free. We raise funds to cover transport, intake fees, and the first year of care for every retired horse.
3. **Partnership with established sanctuaries.** Work with organizations like Catskill Animal Sanctuary, Equine Advocates, and others with existing capacity and track records.
4. **Tribute horses in the game.** Each real horse retired from Central Park service gets a named digital counterpart in the WHO — with unique stats and appearance based on the real horse. This keeps their story alive and gives donors something tangible to connect with.

### Long-term

- Establish or support a WHO-affiliated sanctuary in the Hudson Valley or Catskills region within driving distance of NYC.
- Annual fundraising events (virtual and in-person) to sustain ongoing care.
- Transparent public reporting on every horse: where they are, how they're doing, photos and updates.

## Plan for the workers

This is critical. If we don't have a credible answer for the drivers, the campaign fails politically.

### Electric carriages

- Same routes through Central Park, same scenic experience for tourists.
- Drivers keep their jobs. Same tips, same customer interaction.
- Electric vehicles are quieter, produce no emissions, and eliminate the animal welfare issue entirely.
- The vintage aesthetic can be preserved — electric carriages can be designed to look classic.
- Companies like Blue Star EV and Westward Industries already make electric utility and shuttle vehicles that could be adapted.

### Transition plan

1. **License conversion.** Existing carriage medallion holders convert to electric carriage permits at no additional cost.
2. **Training program.** City-funded (or WHO-subsidized) training for drivers on electric vehicle operation and maintenance. Should be short — these are experienced drivers.
3. **Vehicle subsidy.** The city and/or private donors (including WHO fundraising) subsidize the purchase of electric carriages for existing license holders.
4. **Timeline.** 18-24 month wind-down period. Existing horse licenses sunset gradually while electric permits ramp up. No cliff — a smooth transition.

## Legislative landscape (as of April 2026)

- **Ryder's Law (Intro 967):** Named after a carriage horse that collapsed and died in Hell's Kitchen in 2022. Introduced by Councilmember Robert Holden. Would phase out horse carriage licenses and replace them with electric alternatives.
- **Committee defeat (Nov 2025):** The Health Committee voted it down 4-1 with 2 abstentions. It was forced to a vote via procedural maneuver after Council leadership refused to bring it to the floor.
- **17 Council co-sponsors** as of last count.
- **Executive Order 56 (Sep 2025):** Mayor Adams signed an EO strengthening oversight and preparing for an eventual ban, but the legislative path stalled.
- **New mayor (Mamdani, Jan 2026):** Has taken a "deeper study" position. Not hostile but not actively championing it yet.
- **Key orgs:** NYCLASS (primary advocacy), Animal Legal Defense Fund, PETA, Central Park Conservancy (recently came out against carriages).

### What needs to happen

1. Get Ryder's Law reintroduced in the new Council term.
2. Build a broader coalition — the Central Park Conservancy's support is new and significant.
3. Pressure the new mayor to take an active position.
4. Neutralize TWU opposition by publicly and credibly solving the worker transition.
5. Fundraise to support lobbying, sanctuary placement, and electric carriage pilots.

## How WHO contributes

The WHO game is free-to-play with optional microtransactions (players buy in-game credits). Credits never convert to real money -- this is a game, not a gambling platform. **Profits from credit purchases fund the WHO real-world mission.** The first mission is ending horse-drawn carriages in Central Park.

This means every player who buys credits is directly funding horse rescue, whether they know it or not. The revenue split (operational costs vs. mission fund) will be published on the site.
